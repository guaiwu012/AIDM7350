AI content creation / synthetic media / election misinformation

When a cloned voice enters a real election

Generative AI has made content creation faster, cheaper, and more believable. The same systems that help people create images, writing, and audio can also produce persuasive misinformation. This site focuses on one case: the 2024 New Hampshire robocall that used an AI-generated voice resembling Joe Biden to discourage participation in the Democratic primary.

The argument of this website is simple: AI misinformation is not only dangerous because it creates fake content. It becomes dangerous when synthetic content enters real communication systems, reaches real people, and exploits trust at a moment when verification is difficult.

Why this topic matters

AI in content creation is often discussed through productivity, creativity, and personalization. Those are real benefits. But the same features can also lower the cost of deception. Synthetic audio matters especially because a voice feels personal, immediate, and authoritative. In an election context, that combination can make one false message feel urgent enough to act on.

Section 1 / Storytelling

The New Hampshire robocall case

What happened

In the several weeks preceding the 2024 democratic primary in New Hampshire, some of the registered voters were contacted by pre-recorded messages, with a synthetic voice that sounded like the voice of the President Joe Biden. There, the communications were directed to not vote in the primary election, but to save their participation in the election in general in November. The success of this type of trick was not only due to any technological possibility, but to the coincidence of three important things: the recognition of the characteristics of the voice of a important political figure, the use of a communication that is instant, and the time in which it was taken, coinciding with an important process electoral. This is why the case is useful for a class website. It is easy to explain, but it opens broader questions about AI-generated audio, public trust, platform responsibility, election integrity, and media literacy. The case also has a full arc: the call appeared, journalists and officials investigated it, regulators responded, and later legal outcomes complicated the public story.

This particular case is a very good didactic for the purpose of instruction for the following reasons. First, it is easy to describe but it involves several serious concerns about the present many issues: the veracity of the sound recordings that were made in a artificial way, loss of confidence in the public institutions, the responsibility of the communication platforms, the protection of the electoral process, and the public awareness of the techniques of digital manipulations. Moreover, the situation follows a complete story of progress: first, the dissemination of the falsified content; then, the questions by the news and the government authorities; then, the regulations taken; and at the end, the legal developments that also made the situation more and more complicated for the public understanding of the events.

The key point is that the misleading information can have its effect without any fancy visual at all. A short voice recording that was sent by standard telephone conversation could be enough to shape the public perception of the political processes. Many of the electors consider that automated calls from political leaders with whom they are familiar are by nature more trustworthy than digital communications such as tweets or electronic mail, because it exploits the older credibility associated to a verbal exchange as a means of intimate and immediate contact. In contrast to the manipulated video contents that require a concentrated attention, the prerecorded political messages do not require citizens to be involved at all - these can be received when you commute, when you cook, when you make other daily activities, when there is simply a little bit of opportunity to analytically evaluate before the content gets in. This is so easy to spread, the synthetic voice technology is an extremely dangerous instrument for an electoral manipulation: the way it is implemented it requires little technical expertise for the wide spread, it can target in particular way the voting groups with particular contents, and it creates a wide spread uncertainty that is much more difficult to solve than a viral social media post serves as the focal point.

Why this case special

What sets this case apart from it being just another case of making up audio is that it is using some kind of normal communication channels. Instead of, for example, the common scams which send people to strange websites or make them download something dubious, this fake call came through something that is known and recognized. This is important because people will relax their guard when they are using some kind of regular communication. The situation shows how making up material can get some kind of authenticity not only from its content, but also from using some kind of usual systems.

The technology that we have used for these automated calls was not some very secret or illegal service; it was a common, easily available technology that is regularly used by political campaigns and by others who have to talk to voters or clients. The deceiving call was therefore not caught by many of the security measures or by the caution of the users that one can give to strange links on the web or to unknown applications. So, many that would ignore an unsolicited political email would answer a call of a local number thinking that is a real call to talk with them.

The fact that it is becoming more and more hard to discriminate what is a respectable campaign strategy from what is harmful as misinformation is a serious question about the role of artificial intelligence in the electoral processes. This is a problem for the regulatory bodies that have to keep in balance the protection of the democratic discourse and the use and security of the elections also allowing for political outreach activities.

Why timing mattered

The power of the electoral falsehoods depends on the right time to do it. A fake communication can be powerful without being online for a long time, if it is aimed at the right people at the right time just before the effort to fact check will have enough power. The example of New Hampshire is good because the false robocall is spread in the last two days before the primary. This is a very short time for the local election authority and news organizations to have the time to spread the big correction before the citizens cast their vote.

In fact, even if voters later found that the call was fraud, the planted uncertainty could still have been there, which could have led to misperceptions on the electoral procedures and to doubts on the integrity of the voting system. Because of the temporal aspect of the fact, the reactions of both media and of the regulators were bigger, and the event that could be just a local fact, became a country wide debate on the problem of the use of artificial intelligence in order to deception, on the electoral integrity and the necessity of updating the protective means against the new technological risks to the democratic institutions.

Section 2 / Key concepts

AI in content creation and misinformation

Concept overview

The New Hampshire robocall case is not only a story about one fake message. It sits at the intersection of synthetic media, voice cloning, election misinformation, infrastructure abuse, and public trust. Generative AI lowers the cost of producing realistic content, but real harm emerges when that content enters familiar systems, borrows authority, and reaches people at a moment when verification is difficult.

Section 3 / Media and research

How coverage and scholarship frame the issue

This section can support the “review analysis” part of your assignment.

Media coverage

What the news emphasized

News coverage of the robocall case generally moved through three frames. First, it treated the incident as a breaking political story: what happened, who was involved, and how the robocalls reached voters. Second, coverage shifted toward regulation and accountability, focusing on how officials identified the source and how the FCC responded. Third, some reporting framed the case as a broader sign of how AI-generated media could affect future elections.

This matters because media reports do more than document facts. They also shape what audiences think the problem actually is: a technical trick, a democratic threat, or a governance challenge.

Research review

What existing research helps explain

Research on deepfakes and synthetic media helps explain why this case is more than a one-off scandal. Studies suggest that people are not consistently good at identifying AI-generated political media, especially when the content already fits prior expectations or appears in a low-attention setting. Other work points to a second-order harm: once audiences know convincing synthetic media exists, trust can erode even toward genuine recordings.

For this website, the most useful research themes are not technical detection benchmarks. They are questions of discernment, trust, credibility, and governance.

Key idea 1

Detection is unreliable in everyday use, so audiences cannot be expected to solve the problem by intuition alone.

Key idea 2

Even when a fake is later exposed, the experience can still damage trust in political communication.

Key idea 3

The challenge is social as well as technical: regulation, infrastructure, journalism, and literacy all matter.

Suggested paragraph for your report

Taken together, media coverage and research suggest that AI misinformation should not be treated as only a content problem. The New Hampshire case shows how synthetic audio can exploit the credibility of familiar voices and ordinary distribution systems, while research on deepfakes indicates that audiences are not consistently able to detect such manipulation. This means that the harm of AI-generated misinformation lies not only in false content itself, but in the broader weakening of informational trust.

Section 4 / Why it matters

Impact beyond “did people believe it?”

Individual level

At the personal level, artificial audio can give the feeling of closeness and of credibility. For a routine concrete call on the phone, a person might not check that much about the things he receives, especially if it feels pressed or if this is something he can recognize. In the case of the New Hampshire primary, the fake call took advantage of the fact that it was an election reminder, which is in itself time sensitive, and also that he was calling a current president, which is something the person can recognize, so that it bypassed the rational scrutiny. Many of the recipients said that they felt more like of bewilderment than of instant doubt, because the content was like the communication of campaign. Such a disorientation can have concrete negative consequences: that eligible voters may not take part, may make mistake in the vote they chose, or develop doubts about his capacity to take part in the democratic process, even if after he finds out that the call was fake.

Social level

Of this kind of instances, the societal price of credibility can be raised. If people will learn that audio recordings can be made in a synthetic way, then authentic evidence in the voice might as well be considered as such. This is a "penalty of credibility" that will propagate on the society networks: authentic political communications, important public warnings, private voice communications from family members might all be refused as possible synthetic media. Concerning the electoral processes, such a skepticism would make citizens to not believe of all messages related to the campaign, from the details of the voting sites, to the items of the referendums, to the position of the political candidates, since one can worry that it can be manipulated. Then, slowly, it is the common ground of civic dialogue that is being undermined, making more difficult to the communities to establish an agreement on the most important democratic principles.

Institutional level

Effective and trustworthy information exchange is prerequisite for democratic governance. Deceptive artificially generated communications may force the bodies in charge of oversight, the news organizations, and the communication networks to react faster than the running of their operational framework would permit. In the case of the New Hampshire incident, the electoral authorities, the telecommunications companies, and the police agencies had to organize an immediate countermeasure in the last moment before the election was to be held, and to move out of the normal running of the electoral operations the personnel and the assets of these institutions. Such institutional mechanisms are put in the shape of being carefully and methodically supervised, and not of being instantaneously protected against fake devises of the AI. The effect of a constant attack is to deplete the capabilities of the organizations, and produce at a part of the time a set of vulnerabilities that the bad guys could use in the next cycle of the elections, in order to slowly erode the trust of the citizens on the basic legitimacy of the democratic systems.

Myth vs fact

Myth: If it sounds real, it is real.

Fact: Audio realism does not prove authenticity. A familiar voice can be generated, edited, or strategically deployed.

Myth: Only viral misinformation matters.

Fact: A targeted message can still matter if it reaches the right audience at the right time.

Myth: Once a fake is exposed, the harm is over.

Fact: Trust may already be weakened before the correction reaches everyone.

Section 5 / Response

Response has to be layered, not one-dimensional

Core idea

By the time misinformation is exposed, some of the damage has often already happened. A cloned voice may already have sounded believable, a false message may already have reached people at exactly the wrong moment, and a correction may still be moving too slowly.

That is why the response cannot be reduced to “just be careful.” Effective response has to work at several levels at once: users need stronger habits of verification, platforms and media need systems that label, trace, and correct harmful content, and policy needs accountability plus faster reporting pathways.

The short version: synthetic misinformation becomes powerful when fake media combines with timing, emotion, platform distribution, and public trust.
